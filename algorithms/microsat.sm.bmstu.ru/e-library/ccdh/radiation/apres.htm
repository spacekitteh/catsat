RELIABILITY PREDICTION AND IMPROVEMENT OF ELECTRONIC SYSTEMS ON-BOARD MODERN COST-EFFECTIVE MICROSATELLITES.

Veronica Asenek, Prof. Martin Sweeting, Dr. Jeff Ward.

Centre for Satellite Engineering Research,

University of Surrey, Guildford, GU2 5XH, UK

Tel: +44 1483 259278 Fax: +44 1483 259503 Email: v.asenek@ee.surrey.ac.uk

Abstract: The Centre for Satellite Engineering Research (CSER) and Surrey Satellite Technology Limited (SSTL) working together have established themselves as pioneers in low cost small satellite technology. One of the biggest challenges of reducing the high cost of space missions is maintaining high reliability. Research undertaken at CSER has shown that there is currently no suitable method for predicting the reliability of electronic systems on-board modern cost-effective small satellites. This research also revealed that it can be very difficult to improve existing methods or develop a new methodology which could be used practically for this purpose. However, it was found that the quality and consequently reliability of modern electronic components is currently very high. Furthermore, if the "top-down" approach to design, as well as current reliability improvement techniques and tools, are used appropriately in the design an electronic system, then it is very unlikely that such a system will fail.

 

  1. Introduction

Because of the high cost involved in space missions, all customers in the space industry want to know how much risk is involved with a new product before purchasing it. For this reason, there is great need for a methodical way to accurately predict the reliability of spacecraft. This problem has been a fundamental issue in the space industry since the early 1950s. Dealing exclusively with electronic hardware reliability, research was undertaken at CSER to establish a methodology for:

 

  • accurately predicting the reliability of electronic systems on small modern low cost spacecraft, such that alternative designs could be compared,

  • identifying potential weaknesses in a design,

taking into consideration the unique properties of these spacecraft.

System reliability prediction begins by developing a reliability block diagram (model) of the system. The reliability model takes into consideration the amount of redundancy built into the system. The basic input into this model is the reliability value of the system’s components. Two approaches for predicting the reliability of electronic components were identified: an empirical based prediction methodology (e.g. MIL-HDBK-217) and a science based (theoretical) approach known as the Physics-of-Failure (PoF) approach.

Section 3 of this paper presents the characteristics of a typical UoSAT (University of Surrey Satellite) spacecraft. Sections 4 and 5 describe MIL-HDBK-217 method and the physics-of-failure approach respectively. Section 6 discusses the application of MIL-HDBK-217 and PoF approach to small modern low cost spacecraft. It also outlines recommendations of how more reliable systems could be realised. Section 7 present tools and techniques that can be used to implement these recommendations followed by a conclusion in section 8.

 

  1. Characteristics of a Typical UoSAT Spacecraft

Figure-1 shows an exploded view of a typical UoSAT microsatellite. The internal part of the spacecraft consists of a number of stacked aluminium trays1,2. Each tray supports an electronic system whose reliability may be essential to the success of the whole mission.

Figure-1: An exploded view of a UoSAT microsatellite

Typical satellite characteristics include:

  1. They are manufactured from low-cost modern components from rapidly emerging technologies for which failure rate data currently does not exist.

  2. Each satellite typically consists of about thirty thousand components.

  3. Typical flight mass is around 50kg.

  4. An average mission costs ~£2.0 million.

  5. Average manufacture and development time from proposal to launch is 12 months.

  6. They are flown in Low Earth Orbit (LEO) and are not in continuous contact with every point on earth, consequently, they employ a store-and-forward transponder for communication2. The transponder stores received signals on-board the satellites and then transmits them when it comes in contact with a ground station. As a result, these satellites have on-board solid state high capacity memory devices for data storage. Most of the devices have no flight heritage and are quite complex with high capability and speed.

  7. These spacecraft also use complex and flexible on-board computing systems based on microprocessors, again often without flight heritage.

 

 

 

  1. MIL-HDBK-217

MIL-HDBK-217 (Military Handbook, Reliability Prediction of Electronic Equipment)3, is the most widely used tool for predicting component reliability. The first version of the handbook (MIL-HDBK-217A) was published in 1965, followed by updated versions in subsequent years to reflect the change in technology. MIL-HDBK-217F is the latest version. The content of MIL-217F includes failure rate models for nineteen major electronic component categories used in modern systems, ranging from microcircuits and discrete semiconductors to passive components such as resistors and capacitors. Models are developed by fitting curves to historical failure data from field operation or from laboratory tests. The handbook contains two methods for performing component reliability prediction, the parts count and parts stress methods4.

An example of a parts stress model, for a microcircuits is given as follows:

l p=(C1p T+ C2p E)p Qp L

where

l p = Part failure rate;

C1 = Die base failure rate, based on the circuit complexity (e.g. number of gates);

C2 = Package base failure rate, based on package type and complexity (e.g. pin count);

p T = Temperature acceleration factor for die related mechanisms (e.g. electromigration, time dependent dielectric breakdown);

p E = Environmental factor which accounts for all environmental effects except steady state operating temperature (e.g. thermal cycling, vibration, humidity);

p Q = Quality factor which accounts for the level of screening and processing of the device;

p L = Learning factor which accounts for the maturity of the device manufacturing line.

Unfortunately, MIL-HDBK-217 models have been highly criticised, and some critics have even called for its elimination. Examples of issues that have been raised against the handbook models include:

  • The models do not address all the possible causes of failure5,6. They assume that fundamental part design is perfect and that failures are caused only by manufacturing defects which are accelerated by operational stresses.

  • The reliability of any electronic equipment depends on how it is designed, manufactured, stored, transported and used. All these processes vary from company to company and as such introduce large variability in the data collected from different companies. This variability is not accounted for in MIL-HDBK-217 models7.

  • It is possible that the failure data is affected by external failure mechanisms and flawed reporting techniques. Consequently, there is no statistical confidence associated with results obtained using the models8.

  • Since MIL-HDBK-217 models are empirically derived, they do not identify the actual origin of failures and therefore cannot be used for identifying potential reliability problems.

  • The models are developed by assuming that the failure rate of components is constant9. This implies that the reliability of a component is independent of its age. Although this assumption is made on the basis that equipment will start its mission after the early failures have been eliminated and completes the mission before wear-out sets in (as described by the bath-tube curve), experience has proved otherwise.

  • The handbook also assumes that failure rate and reliability are related by the Arrhenius relationship. That is, the tendency for the component to fail increases with temperature. Evidence has also suggested otherwise7,10.

  • Most of the models are reported to be out of date and are therefore not applicable for state-of-the-art components11.

  • Finally, where enough data is not available, models are obtained by extrapolating from available data. The results obtained in such situations have been described by some authors as educated guesses12.

The lack of data is a growing problem as the reliability of components continue to increase (Figure-2). With the modern production techniques available today, the mean time between failure (MTBF) for components is currently very high13. It is necessary to test thousand of components for months and possibly years to obtain sufficient data to develop accurate empirical models. It is forecast that, at the turn of the century, tens of thousands of components will be tested at accelerated conditions for many weeks with no failures occurring14. Lack of data is in fact an "acute" problem as far as empirical models are concerned.

 

Figure-2: Quality/Reliability Evolution in Government and Industrial Parts15.

Despite these weaknesses, MIL-HDBK-217 is still the most widely used tool for making reliability predictions for complex electronic systems and for comparing design options. This is mainly because the handbook models are the only models that are available, and are well documented and easy to use for making reliability predictions. Software packages facilitating their application to complex systems are even available. Manufacturers of spacecraft and other complex electronic systems therefore have no choice but to use MIL-HDBK-217 when reliability analysis is requested by the customer. Other empirical reliability prediction handbooks exist but similar controversies surrounds them15, 16,17.

According to Jensen14, "Achieving high reliability is a process of sound design and manufacturing practices. Using handbook predictions for design comparisons is rarely a good idea". It can mislead the designer to select a less reliable component over a more reliable one due to the lack of coherence between predicted values. Table-1 illustrates this point with predictions for a commercial-grade, plastic, 64K DRAM under the same environmental conditions, using empirical based models from different handbooks.

In general, empirical models have become inaccurate due to insufficient statistics on the failure of components. Critics of empirical models prefer the science based method known as the physics-of-failure (PoF) approach20.

  1. Physics of Failure (PoF) approach

Cushing et al19, defined this as an approach to design, reliability assessment, testing, screening and stress margins that uses knowledge of root-cause failure processes to prevent product failures through rigorous design and manufacturing practices. To apply it, the following steps are taken:

  • Identify potential failure mechanisms (e.g. chemical, electrical, physical, mechanical, structural, or thermal processes leading to failure) and the failure sites on each device.

  • Develop a model for each failure mechanism through established principles of physics, chemistry, mechanics and materials. Input parameters to these models include characteristics of the materials of the device, geometry at the failure site, manufacturing flaws, defects and environmental and operating loads.

  • Analyse all potential failure mechanisms at each failure site and determine its mean time to failure (MTTF). The mechanism with the shortest calculated life is taken as the MTTF of the components and consequently of the system.

The main disadvantages of this approach include:

  • The POF approach is focus on basic component design reliability and does not address external causes of failure possible causes of failure20.

  • The validation of each failure mechanism can only be done through experimentation by multiple researchers, since it is a very specific and complex approach. This approach is therefore very expensive20.

  • It requires a lot of expertise and machinery to conduct the necessary tests required to determine the parameters in the models.

  • Models are currently not available for all categories of electronic components.

However, there are many advantages to this approach, although most of them are of benefit to manufacturers of particular devices4,18,20. Some of these include:

  • Since PoF approach explicitly address the parameters which are found to strongly influence component reliability (material properties, electrical, thermal, mechanical and chemical stresses), it is a useful tool for designing components.

  • It establishes a scientific basis for evaluating new materials, structures, and electronic technologies. This approach can therefore be used for predicting the reliability of components developed from new technology.

  • By identifying and modelling potential failure mechanisms, it provides information that enables the manufacturer to plan tests and screen parts more effectively, as well as allowing them to determine electrical and thermal-mechanical stress margins.

Despite these advantages, it is still reasonable to conclude that this approach is unsuitable for low cost applications because of its complexity and consequently the cost involved.

 

  1. Conclusions on Component Reliability Prediction Methods

Firstly, since empirical models are inaccurate, they cannot be used to compare alternative designs reliably. Unfortunately, it is difficult to improve the accuracy of these models since the reliability of components continues to increase and technology changes rapidly. Secondly, the PoF approach is very complex and models do not currently exist for all classes of components. It is therefore impractical for application to the whole spacecraft.

From this knowledge, we believe that neither of these methods are suitable for predicting the reliability of modern components and hence the reliability of electronic sub-systems on modern low cost spacecraft. Consequently, reliability prediction is an ineffective method for comparing alternative designs of modern electronic systems. Experienced from the design, manufacture and operation of 11 microsatellites by CSER and SSTL have identified the "top down" approach defined by O’Connor21 as a better method of achieving higher reliability in electronic systems. Using the ‘top down’ approach (from system to component level) together with engineering judgement, critical areas (weaknesses) in a design could be identified and weighting factors assigned to them. These weighting factors can be used as a basis for comparing alternative designs. Example of critical areas include:

  • single point failures,

  • areas in which design changes have been implemented,

  • areas where new components or non-space qualified parts have been used.

To further improve the design, reliability improvement techniques could then be applied to these areas, lessening the probability of the system failing.

 

  1. Reliability Improvement Techniques

    Reliability improvement techniques include techniques of fault prevention and fault tolerance (Figure-3)22,23. Fault prevention is the traditional approach for increasing the reliability of a system. In involves ensuring that all possible causes of unreliability have been removed from the system before it is put to service. This can be achieved using two basic techniques, fault avoidance and fault removal.

    Fault avoidance is concerned with the use of design and manufacturing methodologies aimed to avoid the introduction of faults into a system. Fault removal is concerned with checking the completed system design and removing any faults that are found22. Examples of tools which can be used for this purpose include FMECA (Failure Mode Effect and Criticality Analysis), FTA (Fault Tree Analysis) and SCA (Sneak Circuit Analysis)13.

    Since unexpected failures may still occur in a system, fault tolerance (redundancy) techniques can be used to make a design more robust. That is, being able to operate successfully with the failure of some of its parts. Two main redundancy techniques were identified: active redundancy and stand-by redundancy. Both techniques can be further classified into sub-divisions also shown in Figure-3.

     

  2. Conclusions

This research has shown that none of the methods currently available is suitable for accurately predicting the reliability of state-of-the-art electronic components and hence the reliability of electronic sub-systems on modern small cost-effective spacecraft. Since technology is rapidly changing, modern production techniques have resulted in extremely low statistics of failure in electronic components. As a result, empirical reliability prediction models (e.g. MIL-HDBK-217) which are widely used have become inaccurate. Attempting to improve existing models or develop new ones is practically impossible considering the time and cost constraints associated with low cost missions. The alternative theoretical based physics-of-failure approach tends to be very complex and time consuming when applied to complex systems. It was found, however that higher system reliability can be realised using the "top down" approach to design together with engineering judgement to identify and weigh critical areas. Alternative designs could then be compared on the basis of the number of critical areas and their weighting factors. To further reduce the chances of failure, reliability improvement techniques are implemented in these critical areas.
